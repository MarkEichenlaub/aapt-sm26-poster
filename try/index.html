Survey a scenarioPhysics 1: Mechanics · Circular Motion

Heavier, lighter, or normal?

A car drives along a hilly road at constant speed (side view below). At each labeled point, does the driver feel heavier than, the same as, or lighter than their normal weight? Drag each label to a bin (or tap the label, then the bin).

What students are doing: reading curvature off a picture. Valley bottoms curve upward — the road must push extra hard, so the driver feels heavier. Hilltops curve away — lighter. Straight stretches, even slanted ones, add nothing. No numbers are computed, but the answer is graded instantly.
Interpret graphsPhysics 1: Mechanics · Forms of Energy

A peregrine falcon dives

A falcon dives from rest, approaching terminal velocity as air drag builds. Connect each plot to the quantity being plotted. Drag from a plot to a quantity (or tap one, then the other). Tap a wire to remove it.

What students are doing: translating a physical story into qualitative features of curves. Speed grows and saturates. The magnitude of acceleration starts at g and dies away. Height falls ever faster, then at a steady rate.
Predict an experimentIntroduction to Physics · Conservation Laws

Where does the pendulum stop?

A pendulum is released from rest at the right-hand end of the dotted arc (shown faintly) and swings back and forth along it. A thin peg is suddenly inserted directly beneath the pivot. When the string hits the peg, the peg becomes the new pivot. Click on the point where the bob stops and turns around. Your click marks a small circle: you're claiming the stopping point is inside it. (Assume an ideal pendulum.)

What students are doing: using energy conservation to make a spatial commitment. The bob must return to its release height — but on the tighter circle around the peg. Clicking the no-peg stopping point is the classic near-miss: right conservation law, wrong constraint. A grader that accepts a click can tell those two ideas apart.
Measure the world from a photoPhysics 1: Mechanics · Projectile Motion

Fit the fountain

This fountain shoots water across the Chicago River, and you want to figure out how fast the water is being shot out (a follow-up problem computes the speed from your fit). Axes are overlaid in units of 10 m. Adjust the coefficients of your (red) parabola: y/10 m = a·(x/10 m)² + b. You will not be able to fit the entire arc — the stream is not a perfect parabola. Think about which part of the water's path it's most important to match, and fit that part as well as you can.

What students are doing: fitting a model against reality — and deciding where the model deserves to fit. By the left side of the arc, air drag has pushed the water well off any parabola, so the freshly-launched right side is the part to match. The graded answer is two numbers with tolerance; the activity is physical judgment.
Reason with symmetryMiddle School Physics 1 · Sight and Images

Letters in a camera obscura

A camera obscura (pinhole in a dark room) projects an image of Times Square onto the wall — upside down and left–right reversed. Which of these letters would look identical to normal in the projected image? Select all that apply. (Assume the most symmetric font for each letter.)

A E F H Z
What students are doing: discovering that "flipped up–down and left–right" is the same as rotating 180° — so a letter survives exactly when it has two-fold rotational symmetry. Mentally rotating letters is the whole task; the finite answer set is what makes it gradable.
Interpret an expressionIntroduction to Physics · Dimensional Analysis

Length, area, or volume?

Every red variable below is a length of some kind — a might be a side length, r a radius. (π is just a number; it has no dimensions.) You don't need to know where these expressions come from: just from how the lengths are added, multiplied, squared, and square-rooted, classify each expression as a length, an area, or a volume. Drag each expression to a bin (or tap it, then the bin).

What students are doing: reading mathematical structure without context — dimensional analysis as a way to make sense of formulas you've never seen. (These are real formulas: a 3-D diagonal, a triangle's area, a frustum's surface, a cell biologist's estimate of the endoplasmic reticulum's area…)
