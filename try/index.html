Survey a scenarioPhysics 1: Mechanics · Circular Motion

Heavier, lighter, or normal?

A car drives along a hilly road at constant speed (side view below). At each labeled point, does the driver feel heavier than, the same as, or lighter than their normal weight? Tap a label, then tap a bin.

What students are doing: reading curvature off a picture. Valley bottoms (B, E) curve upward — the road must push extra hard, so the driver feels heavier. Hilltops (C) curve away — lighter. Straight stretches (A, D), even slanted ones, add nothing. No numbers are computed, but the answer is graded instantly.
Interpret graphsPhysics 1: Mechanics · Forms of Energy

A peregrine falcon dives

A falcon dives from rest, approaching terminal velocity as air drag builds. Connect each plot to the quantity being plotted. Tap a plot, then tap a quantity. Tap a wire to remove it.

What students are doing: translating a physical story into qualitative features of curves. Speed grows and saturates. The magnitude of acceleration starts at g and dies away. Height falls ever faster, then at a steady rate. Nothing to plug in — and only one matching survives.
Commit to a predictionIntroduction to Physics · Conservation Laws

Where does the pendulum stop?

Galileo releases a pendulum from point C. Swinging through the bottom B, the string catches on a peg at E, and the lower part swings on around it. Click the point in the image where the bob stops and turns around. (The point you're clicking is not necessarily labeled. Assume an ideal pendulum.)

What students are doing: using energy conservation to make a spatial commitment. The bob must return to its release height — but on the tighter circle around the peg E. Clicking D (same height, original arc) is the classic near-miss: right conservation law, wrong constraint. A grader that accepts a click can tell those two ideas apart.
Reason with symmetryMiddle School Physics · Sight and Images

Letters in a camera obscura

A camera obscura (pinhole in a dark room) projects an image of Times Square onto the wall — upside down and left–right reversed. Which of these letters would look identical to normal in the projected image? Select all that apply. (Assume the most symmetric font for each letter.)

A E F H Z
What students are doing: discovering that "flipped up–down and left–right" is the same as rotating 180° — so a letter survives exactly when it has two-fold rotational symmetry. H and Z do; A, E, and F don't. Mentally rotating letters is the whole task; the finite answer set is what makes it gradable.
